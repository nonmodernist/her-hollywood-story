America's Sweetheart Chooses Her Stories: Mary Pickford and American Women Writers

How the Queen of the Movies used women's stories to build her empire

Between 1910 and 1927, Mary Pickford starred in 18 films adapted from 14 American women authors whose works spanned fifty years of literature. These adaptations reveal something far more remarkable than a filmography: they chart Pickford's transformation from stock company player to multimedia mogul.

Through strategic choices—from the 1914 film that launched her stardom to the 1922 remake that showcased her creative control, from photoplay editions that carried her image into America's bookstores to licensing her catalog to Shirley Temple—Pickford didn't just adapt women's literature. She transformed it into the foundation of an entertainment empire that transcended the silent screen itself.

18 films • 50 years of literature • A singular legacy

From Gaslight to Spotlight: What Pickford Choose and Why

To understand how Mary Pickford used women's literature to build her career, we must first map the territory she claimed. Her 18 adaptations weren't random selections from bestseller lists—they reveal a deliberate strategy bridging two entertainment worlds.

Pickford's choices span an extraordinary fifty years of American women's literature, from Frances Hodgson Burnett's "Esmeralda" (1877) to Kathleen Norris's My Best Girl (published and filmed in 1927). This range positioned Pickford as cultural translator: she brought forward the melodramatic traditions that had trained her on stage—the threatened innocence, the plucky poverty, the triumphant virtue—while also picking contemporary stories that spoke to modern audiences. She was a bridge between the gaslight and the spotlight.

Burnett's heroines, who emerged from the periodical press to become famous onstage, offered Pickford a theatrical tradition perfectly suited to silent film: children and young women whose essential goodness transforms the adults around them. These were melodramas of class mobility, where virtue literally pays. Sara Crewe maintains her princess-like dignity in poverty; Cedric Errol's American democracy softens British aristocracy; even adult Glad in The Dawn of a Tomorrow brings light to London's slums through sheer optimism.

Crucially, Pickford aged into these roles rather than out of them. She played Sara Crewe at 25 and dual roles as both Cedric and his mother "Dearest" at 29—using Victorian literature's idealized children to extend her own screen childhood indefinitely. While Burnett would eventually have 18 films in Hollywood's adaptation catalog (1913-1949), it was Pickford who established her as screen-worthy, transforming stage melodrama's gestural emotion into cinema's intimate art.

From Belasco to Biograph and Beyond: The Stage Training That Shaped a Star

Pickford's affinity for Victorian melodrama wasn't accidental—it was bred in her bones through years of theatrical training. At age eight, she toured in stock companies performing the melodramas that Burnett and her contemporaries had written. By fourteen, she had graduated to David Belasco's Broadway company, where the legendary producer taught her the art of emotional restraint within spectacle.

When D.W. Griffith hired her at Biograph in 1909, Pickford got the chance to learn cinema from one of its masters. By 1917, she was teaching directors her own innovations. During the filming of The Pride of the Clan, Kevin Brownlow recounts, Pickford convinced Maurice Tourneur to adopt stage lighting techniques: "One morning she had an idea that a 'baby spot,' a lighting technique used in the theater to pick out a character’s face or hands, might brighten a face on film to suggest that the character was extremely young." Tourneur initially declined the suggestion, but Pickford persisted—and when Tourneur saw the results, he admitted she was right.

This theatrical lineage—from touring melodramas through Belasco's Broadway to Griffith's close-ups—explains why Pickford gravitated toward authors like Burnett. She was translating an entire performance tradition she had mastered since childhood, bringing the emotional technology of nineteenth-century theater into the twentieth century's newest medium.

Seizing the Contemporary Moment

But Pickford was equally adept at seizing the contemporary moment. The same year she produced A Little Princess from 1887, she also grabbed Eleanor Hoyt Brainerd's How Could You, Jean? only one year after its 1917 publication. Most remarkably, she filmed The Eagle's Mate and My Best Girl in the same years they were published (1914 and 1927 respectively)—suggesting her studios were reading galleys, following serializations, or had insider publishing knowledge.

This dual strategy—Victorian sentiment and contemporary currency—positioned Pickford uniquely. She wasn't just adapting books; she was managing time itself, bringing forward what was worth preserving from the past while grabbing what mattered from the present.

"Tess" as a Turning Point

Seven films featuring Pickford were released in 1914, but Tess of the Storm Country transformed her career. Tess allowed Pickford to unleash something primal on screen. As Eileen Whitfield observes, "her Tess is so commanding, so elemental, that she seems to have leaped, fully formed, from the celluloid itself."

The role synthesized everything Pickford had learned: Victorian melodrama's dramatic lamentations, Belasco's emotional restraint, Griffith's intimate camera, and her own fierce intelligence about screen performance. Tess made Pickford an international star and gave her, as Christel Schmidt notes, "incredible bargaining power" that would reshape her career trajectory.

For the next year, the trade press buzzed with rumors that Pickford would leave Adolph Zukor's Famous Players. An unnamed company was reported to have offered her $6,000 a week. But in January 1916, Motion Picture News confirmed that Pickford had in fact signed a lucrative contract with Famous Players. The contract included the formation of the Mary Pickford Film Company and a new distribution concern, Artcraft Pictures Corporation, both under the auspices of Famous Players. Pickford reportedly doubled her weekly salary in 1915, and by 1916, she had negotiated for $10,000 a week and 50% of her film profits.

A Company of Her Own

The 1916 arrangement reveals Pickford's sophisticated understanding of film production as a system of interlocking decisions. Having starred in twenty-two features in two and a half years, as Kevin Brownlow notes, she cut her output down to six films annually and established a regular team of creative collaborators, including screenwriter Frances Marion.

Terry Ramsaye's account of the 1916 negotiations, as discussed by Brownlow, positions Pickford as "a canny negotiator" whose deal-making prowess matched Zukor's business acumen. The creation of her own studio and a distribution company for her films represented parallel structures of control. While Zukor's Famous Players-Lasky purchase of Paramount Pictures Corporation would eventually create a vertically-integrated behemoth, Pickford had secured her creative autonomy within that emerging system.

Schmidt, writing for the Women Film Pioneers Project, emphasizes that Pickford "would sign off on every aspect of her productions, from the script to the director," and "was even known to have a hand in editing." During these peak creative years, she produced and starred in The Poor Little Rich Girl, Rebecca of Sunnybrook Farm, A Little Princess, Amarilly of Clothes-line Alley, and How Could You, Jean?, all adapted by Frances Marion from the stories of American women writers.

Pickford in Print: The Photoplay Edition Phenomenon

While Mary Pickford was revolutionizing star contracts and creative control, 1914 also marked another transformation in the relationship between literature and film—one that would prove particularly lucrative for Pickford's chosen stories.

Building on the tradition of illustrating novels adapted for the stage with production photographs, publishers created movie tie-in books—known as photoplay editions—as early as 1912. As rare book collector Arnie Davis has documented, Pickford's 1914 film The Eagle's Mate marked a turning point in this practice: it was Grosset & Dunlap's first deluxe movie tie-in, establishing the template for their silent era photoplay editions, featuring a photographic dust jacket, multiple stills tipped into the pages, and prominent star billing—innovations that would become standard practice for the next fifteen years.

The photoplay editions of Pickford's films reveal another dimension of her stardom: the transformation of books into promotional objects where the actress was touted alongside the original author. These volumes document both media convergence and market dominance—Pickford's image and name sold books just as surely as they sold movie tickets.

Material Evidence of Media Convergence

Of Pickford's 18 literary adaptations, at least 9 were published in photoplay editions. These books present a particular challenge for researchers: most lack publication dates, many survive only in dealer descriptions, and their print runs remain undocumented. What we can examine directly, however, tells us how publishers packaged literature when a major star was attached.

Reading The Eagle's Mate as Object

The Grosset & Dunlap photo drama edition of Anna Alice Chapin's The Eagle's Mate offers our most complete example of early photoplay publishing practices. The physical book reveals a careful orchestration of text and image:

The Packaging: A photographic dust jacket features Pickford in costume, establishing her as the book's primary selling point before a reader even opens the cover. The spine identifies it as a "photo drama edition," creating a distinct category separate from standard reprints.

The Credits: The title page formally acknowledges "Mary Pickford as the heroine" of "the photo play produced by the Famous Players Film Company."

The Visual Program: Six photographic plates are tipped into the text at regular intervals. Pickford appears in every image in dramatic tableaux. The photos reimagine key scenes through Pickford's performance, with captions pointing readers to the corresponding page of the story.

The Paratext: The book's advertising pages promote other Grosset & Dunlap titles, including "Tess of the Storm Country" with Mary Pickford specifically named as its star. This creates a feedback loop: one Pickford vehicle advertises another, building a literary brand around the actress and her adaptation authors.

The Multiplication Effect

The photoplay editions reveal a sophisticated feedback loop between screen and page, though their undated publication makes precise timing difficult to establish. What we can document is their reach: The Eagle's Mate from Grosset & Dunlap featured six production stills and established the template for their deluxe movie tie-ins. By the late 1910s, multiple Pickford vehicles received this treatment: Rebecca of Sunnybrook Farm (8 stills), The Poor Little Rich Girl (6 stills), and How Could You, Jean? (8 stills), among others.

The international reach of these editions demonstrates Pickford's global stardom. Daddy-Long-Legs appeared in both an American Grosset & Dunlap edition with eight stills and a British Hodder & Stoughton paperback with an illustrated cover. My Best Girl received both an A.L. Burt edition with four stills and a British Readers Library hardback—evidence that publishers on both sides of the Atlantic recognized Pickford's selling power.

The 1920 Pollyanna edition from Page, marked as the "49th printing" with sixteen Pickford stills, suggests these books enjoyed multiple print runs over extended periods. Their scarcity today indicates they were read to pieces rather than preserved—commercial objects designed for consumption rather than collection, spreading Pickford's image through bookstores, lending libraries, and private homes long after films left theaters.

The Unknowns

What these books don't tell us is equally important. The objects don't reveal:

  • Whether Pickford or her companies received payment for image rights
  • How photoplay edition sales compared to regular reprints
  • What role, if any, stars played in approving these editions
  • Why some films generated multiple editions while others apparently generated none

The physical books themselves—with their worn dust jackets, shelf wear, and ownership inscriptions—remind us these were objects designed for mass consumption rather than preservation. That any survive at all is remarkable. That they survive in sufficient numbers to document a pattern is invaluable for understanding how early Hollywood monetized stardom across media platforms.

The United Artists Era

The 1919 founding of United Artists marked Pickford's final break from studio control. Her choice for her first UA release speaks volumes: Pollyanna (1920), Eleanor H. Porter's relentlessly optimistic orphan. Where Tess fought poverty with grit, Pollyanna conquered it with gladness.

Tess, Take Two!

In 1922, Pickford did something relatively unprecedented: she remade her own star-making vehicle, Tess of the Storm Country. Eight years separated the two versions, but they might as well have been different centuries. The film industry had transformed: features had grown from five to ten reels, production values had soared, and Pickford herself had evolved from contract player to industry architect.

The differences reveal how far Pickford had traveled. The 1914 version, directed by Edwin S. Porter, captured lightning in a bottle—raw, urgent, and rough around the edges, filmed in just a few weeks. The 1922 version, directed by John S. Robertson (borrowed from Famous Players-Lasky, no less), was a prestige production filmed over months at a constructed fishing village in Chatsworth Lake.

The remake strategy revealed Pickford's understanding of intellectual property long before Hollywood adopted the term. Purchasing the literary rights to Tess from Zukor meant Pickford owned both the performance and the property, transforming Grace Miller White's squatter girl into a renewable resource that could be updated for new audiences with new technology. Where the 1914 version had launched a star, the 1922 version crowned a mogul.

Pickford, Temple, and Time

Pickford's understanding of literary properties as renewable resources extended beyond her own career. By the mid-1930s, retired from acting but still controlling the remake rights to her signature vehicles, she orchestrated another transformation of these women's stories. The buyer was Fox Film Corporation; the beneficiary was their new sensation, Shirley Temple; the properties were the same tales that had built Pickford's empire. The child star would inherit not just the roles but a literary catalog Pickford had spent two decades curating.

Temple made five films from Pickford's collection: Curly Top, an uncredited version of Daddy-Long-Legs; Poor Little Rich Girl, which was "suggested by" Eleanor Gates's story; Rebecca of Sunnybrook Farm, from the Kate Douglas Wiggin novel; The Little Princess, from the magazine serial by Frances Hodgson Burnett; and Miss Annie Rooney, which took its title but little else from Pickford's original script for Little Annie Rooney (not included in the database).

The difference was stark: Pickford played these young characters at ages 25-27, using technique to suggest innocence, while Temple was actually 7-11 years old when she took on these roles.

When the two met in 1943 to discuss a collaboration—Pickford had paid a record $110,000 for the screen rights to Sally Benson's Junior Miss and wanted Temple for a leading role—a photographer captured them together on the set of Since You Went Away. LIFE Magazine published the photo, cruelly labeling them "Hollywood's two greatest has-beens." The magazine noted they were "almost of a size" (Pickford at 50, Temple at 15) and observed that both had "finished great movie careers"—Pickford as "America's Sweetheart," Temple as "the screen's greatest child actress."

The Pickford-Temple project never materialized (though Fox did release a version of Junior Miss in 1945, starring Peggy Ann Garner). The photograph remains as evidence of Hollywood's brutal mathematics: two actresses whose "combined careers almost span the history of the motion-picture industry" (as LIFE noted), reduced to a punchline about size and age.

Yet it also captures something profound: Pickford had transformed the stories of American women writers into a transferable asset that outlived her own stardom. Temple inherited not just roles but a literary legacy. The stars, as LIFE so coldly observed, became "has-beens"; but the stories Pickford had championed would continue cycling through Hollywood for decades to come.
