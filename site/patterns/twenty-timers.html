The Twenty-Timers Club

Five American women whose novels Hollywood couldn't stop adapting

Between 1915 and 1961, these five writers saw their works adapted to film at least twenty times each. Together, they account for 123 films — over 10% of all Hollywood adaptations from American women writers during this period. They weren't just popular; they were Hollywood's most reliable hitmakers.

The Twenty-Timer Timeline

What Made a Twenty-Timer?

Bestseller Status

All five dominated bestseller lists before Hollywood came calling. Their pre-existing audiences guaranteed box office appeal.

Genre Mastery

Each specialized in genres Hollywood loved: mysteries (Rinehart), melodramas (Hurst), epics (Ferber), nature romances (Stratton-Porter), and society comedies (Miller).

Serial Success

Most published in magazines first, building audiences chapter by chapter before the book — and film — appeared.

Business Savvy

These women understood contracts and rights. Stratton-Porter even founded her own production company to control adaptations.

The Studio System Connection

Warner Bros led all studios with 12 adaptations, but individual relationships tell the real story. Gene Stratton-Porter produced 5 films through her own company (GSP Productions). Fannie Hurst spread her works equally among Universal, Cosmopolitan, and Warner Bros. Paramount claimed 8 of Alice Duer Miller's 21 films.

Peak Adaptation Years

Silent Era Dominance

52 of the 123 films were silent, with Mary Roberts Rinehart leading (21 silent films). The transition to talkies didn't slow them down — it opened new opportunities for remakes.

The 1920s Boom

The roaring twenties saw 43 adaptations, the peak decade. Every member was actively adapted, with multiple films per year common.
