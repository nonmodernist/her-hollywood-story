The Twenty-Timers Club

Five American women whose novels Hollywood couldn't stop adapting

Between 1915 and 1961, these five writers saw their works adapted to film at least twenty times each. Together, they account for 125 films — over 10% of all Hollywood adaptations from American women writers during this period. They weren't just popular; they were Hollywood's most reliable hitmakers.

The Twenty-Timer Timeline

What Made a Twenty-Timer?

Bestseller Status

All five dominated bestseller lists before Hollywood came calling. Their pre-existing audiences guaranteed box office appeal.

Genre Mastery

Each specialized in genres Hollywood loved: mysteries (Rinehart), melodramas (Hurst), epics (Ferber), nature romances (Stratton-Porter), and society comedies (Miller).

Serial Success

Most published in magazines first, building audiences chapter by chapter before the book — and film — appeared.

Business Savvy

These women understood contracts and rights. Stratton-Porter even founded her own production company to control adaptations.

The Studio System Connection

Warner Bros led all studios with 12 adaptations, but individual relationships tell the real story. Gene Stratton-Porter produced 5 films through her own company (GSP Productions). Fannie Hurst spread her works equally among Universal, Cosmopolitan, and Warner Bros. Paramount claimed 8 of Alice Duer Miller's 21 films.

Peak Adaptation Years

Silent Era Dominance

54 of the 125 films were silent, with Mary Roberts Rinehart leading (23 silent films). The transition to talkies didn't slow them down — it opened new opportunities for remakes.

The 1920s Boom

The roaring twenties saw 43 adaptations, the peak decade. Every member was actively adapted, with multiple films per year common.

Mary Roberts Rinehart

31 films • 23 works • 44 years

Signature Work: "The Circular Staircase" / "The Bat"

Did Rinehart's mysterious villain "The Bat" inspire the Batman we know today? Bob Kane claimed this 1926 adaptation influenced his creation of the Dark Knight.

Key Achievements

  • Created the "Had-I-But-Known" mystery genre and "The Butler Did It" trope
  • 23 silent films — more than any other Twenty-Timer
  • "The Circular Staircase" adapted 4 times
  • Worked with 23 different studios, including Goldwyn's Eminent Authors Pictures

Fannie Hurst

27 films • 18 works • 43 years

Signature Work: "Imitation of Life"

What was a "Fannie Hurst marriage"? This groundbreaking author lived separately from her husband for years, pioneering a model of independence that shocked 1920s society.

Key Achievements

  • William Randolph Hearst's Cosmopolitan Productions adapted 5 films (1920-1923)
  • "Back Street" remade across 30 years by Universal (1932-1961)
  • Master of the "women's picture" melodrama
  • Frances Marion directed her debut film from a Hurst story

Edna Ferber

24 films • 16 works • 42 years

Signature Work: "Show Boat" (theatrical) / "Giant" (cinematic)

From the set of "Giant," Ferber befriended James Dean in his final role. She co-founded Giant Productions with director George Stevens, taking unprecedented control over the adaptation.

Key Achievements

  • Pulitzer Prize winner for "So Big" (1925)
  • Warner Bros' favorite — 9 films including First National productions
  • Broadway and Hollywood success — "Show Boat" became a Ziegfeld musical
  • Co-founded Giant Productions to control her own adaptation

Gene Stratton-Porter

21 films • 8 works • 43 years

Signature Work: "A Girl of the Limberlost"

After being dissatisfied with Lasky's 1917 "Freckles," Stratton-Porter founded her own studio in 1923. She died just one year later — what heights might she have reached with more time?

Key Achievements

  • Founded Gene Stratton Porter Productions in Los Angeles (1923)
  • Produced 5 of her own adaptations with family collaboration
  • "Freckles" adapted 4 times over 43 years
  • Wrote the scenario for "Girl of the Limberlost" (1924)

Alice Duer Miller

22 films • 13 works • 34 years

Signature Work: "The White Cliffs of Dover"

Miller's poetry reportedly influenced U.S. entry into WWII. Beyond her Hollywood success, Paramount made 8 foreign-language versions of her films — a phenomenon unique among the Twenty-Timers.

Key Achievements

  • "The Charm School" — record 5 adaptations
  • 20-year relationship with Famous Players-Lasky/Paramount (13 films)
  • Suffragist poet and member of the Algonquin Round Table
  • Also wrote screenplays, including adaptation of "Wife vs. Secretary"

About This Data

How We Count

  • Scope: Only American film productions adapted from novels, short stories, and occasionally poems by American women writers (1910-1963)
  • Excluded: British productions, foreign-language versions (though noted when significant), adaptations from plays, and films based on works by non-American authors
  • Twenty-Timer threshold: Authors with 20 or more American film adaptations from their prose fiction
  • Silent era defined: Films released before 1930 are counted as silent films

Studio Attribution

  • Primary credit: We use the producing studio rather than distributor when both are listed
  • Studio evolution: Famous Players-Lasky and Paramount are tracked separately despite the company evolution, as the merger represents a significant industry shift
  • Author-owned studios: Gene Stratton Porter Productions is counted as a distinct entity to highlight author control
  • Subsidiary relationships: Films from Cosmopolitan Productions (Hearst) and First National (later Warner Bros) are credited to the subsidiary when that was the primary production entity

Data Decisions

  • Remake counting: Each new film version counts as a separate adaptation, even if from the same source work
  • Serial films: Multi-part serials released in the same year count as one film
  • Attribution conflicts: When sources disagree, we prioritize AFI Catalog data, followed by contemporary trade publications
  • Edge cases: Alice Duer Miller's foreign-language versions at Paramount are noted but not included in her count of 22 American productions
